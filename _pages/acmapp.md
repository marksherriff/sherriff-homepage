## Candidate's Most Significant Professional Accomplishments

Over more than twenty years in computing education, Prof. Mark Sherriff has made sustained national contributions to computing education through leadership in the ACM SIGCSE community, the development of mentoring and research infrastructure for computing education researchers, and innovative curricular leadership at the University of Virginia.

A major focus of Sherriff's academic work has been strengthening the computing education research (CER) community through mentoring, professional development, and the promotion of rigorous empirical methods. Through multiple National Science Foundation (NSF)-funded projects, he helped create and sustain national initiatives supporting empirical computing education research and researcher development. Sherriff co-authored a comprehensive review of reporting standards and empirical practices in computing education research venues. At the same time, he co-created the Designing Empirical Education Research Studies (DEERS) workshops and related mentoring initiatives that supported computing educators as they developed and conducted their own empirical research studies. These efforts have provided structured mentorship, research training, and collaborative opportunities for educators and emerging researchers from institutions across the United States. 

Along with his DEERS colleagues, Sherriff was awarded a new $2M collaborative grant from the NSF in January 2026 for a project called REPLICATE with the purpose of directly supporting replication in CS education research and creating new frameworks to support replication work in the future. His work has directly contributed to improving the rigor, reproducibility, and accessibility of computing education research while helping expand participation in the research community itself.

Another significant contribution has been Sherriff's leadership in the NSF Showcase program at the ACM SIGCSE Technical Symposium. Through multiple NSF-funded grants, he helped build and sustain a venue that allowed computing education researchers and educators to share developing projects, receive formative feedback, connect with collaborators, and engage directly with NSF program officers. The Showcase has become an established and valued component of the SIGCSE community and has supported the dissemination and growth of computing education innovation nationwide.

Sherriff has also played a sustained leadership role in the ACM SIGCSE community. His service includes election as Secretary of the ACM SIGCSE Board (2025-2028) and membership on the SIGCSE Technical Symposium Steering Committee. He served as Symposium Co-Chair for the 2020 and 2021 ACM Technical Symposium on Computer Science Education, helping guide the conference community during the disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic and supporting the transition to large-scale online conference operations. Once the SIGCSE Technical Symposium was back in-person in 2022, Sherriff continued to host "SIGCSE Coffee Break," an online interview and commentary program designed to support virtual attendees and strengthen community engagement during the symposium. Recordings of the show can be found at [http://sigcsecoffeebreak.org](http://sigcsecoffeebreak.org). He has additionally served in numerous organizational and program leadership roles for SIGCSE and related conferences, including Associate Program Chair, workshop organizer, reviewer, and giving a keynote in 2022 to first-time symposium attendees about how to become involved in SIGCSE.

At the University of Virginia, Sherriff has demonstrated long-term leadership in curriculum innovation and scalable computing education. He was one of the leading architects of the foundation curriculum redesign while serving as associate chair for undergraduate programs. Sherriff also developed large-enrollment software engineering, capstone, and game design courses. As of spring 2026, Sherriff has taught over 10,000 undergraduate students at UVA. His teaching and educational leadership has been recognized through numerous honors, including the IEEE Computer Society Mary Kenneth Keller Computer Science and Engineering Undergraduate Teaching Award and the University of Virginia All-University Teaching Award. In recognition of his sustained contributions to student education and development, he was named the Joseph L. Vaughan Professor of Humanities, an endowed professorship recognizing sustained contributions to student education and development.

These mentoring and workshop efforts have helped broaden participation in computing education research by supporting educators from a wide range of institutions and career stages. Across these activities, Prof. Mark Sherriff's contributions reflect a consistent commitment to advancing computing education not only through teaching and scholarship, but also through the creation of professional communities, mentoring structures, and organizational infrastructure that support the long-term growth of the field.


## Specific Contributions

_NSF Showcase at the ACM SIGCSE Technical Symposium_    
Co-led and sustained the NSF Showcase program through multiple NSF-funded grants, creating a longstanding SIGCSE venue that supported dissemination of emerging computing education projects, researcher networking, mentoring, and direct engagement with NSF program officers.

_Designing Empirical Education Research Studies (DEERS) Workshops_    
Co-created and organized the DEERS workshops and mentoring initiatives, which trained and supported computing educators and researchers in the design and execution of rigorous empirical computing education research studies.

_Systematic Literature Review of Empiricism and Reporting Norms in Computing Education Research_    
Co-authored a comprehensive review of empirical practices and reporting norms in computing education research venues, helping identify gaps in reproducibility and methodological reporting within the field.

_ACM SIGCSE Symposium Leadership During the COVID-19 Pandemic_    
Served as Symposium Co-Chair for the 2020 and 2021 ACM SIGCSE Technical Symposiums, helping guide the computing education community through the transition to large-scale online conference operations during the COVID-19 pandemic.

_SIGCSE Coffee Break Virtual Community Program_    
Created and hosted “SIGCSE Coffee Break,” an online interview and commentary program that expanded engagement opportunities for virtual SIGCSE attendees and strengthened community interaction during and after the pandemic transition.

_REPLICATE NSF Project_    
Co-Principal Investigator on the NSF-funded REPLICATE project, which develops coordinated multi-site replication studies and supporting frameworks to strengthen rigor and reproducibility in computing education research.

_Undergraduate Curriculum Redesign and Large-Scale Computing Education Leadership at UVA_    
Helped lead the redesign of the University of Virginia’s foundational computing curriculum while developing and teaching large-enrollment software engineering, capstone, and game design courses impacting more than 10,000 undergraduate students.
